Monoliths are not bad because they are monoliths.

They become a problem when change gets dangerous.

When a small update creates fear. When one deployment touches too much. When dependencies are hidden. When testing is unreliable. When nobody feels confident changing core behavior. That is when the architecture starts taxing the business.

The better question is: where is the risk concentrated.

Find the areas where coupling is highest. Where changes ripple too far. Where deployments are hardest. Where ownership is unclear. Where the same code supports unrelated concerns. Those are the places to start.

Then refactor in steps.

Extract interfaces. Isolate data access. Move business rules into clearer modules. Create service boundaries only where they help. Improve tests around critical behaviors. Reduce the number of things that must change together.

This is not glamorous work. But it is how systems become safer.

The strongest refactoring plans do not chase ideal diagrams. They improve real changeability. They make the next decision easier than the last one.

That is what good architecture does.

A monolith can become much easier to work in long before it becomes physically split into separate systems.

That matters, because the business gains value early instead of waiting for a perfect endpoint that may never arrive.
